How Predictive Analytics Works

A step‑by‑step roadmap from raw numbers to better business decisions.

Quick visual: as predictions improve, waste shrinks and useful insight grows over time.

Step 1: Collect Past Data

Predictive analytics begins with what you already have: past sales, receipts, or website logs. Even a simple notebook or POS export can be enough.

  • Daily sales totals (donuts sold, coffee cups, sandwiches).
  • Time of day (morning vs. afternoon rush).
  • Day of week and season (weekend vs. weekday, holidays, exam weeks).

Cause–effect: Without past data, there is nothing solid to base a prediction on.

Step 2: Organize It in a Simple Table

Next, you put the data into a clear format — usually a spreadsheet or simple table.

  • One row per day or per shift.
  • Columns for date, items sold, leftovers or waste, and notes (rainy, promotion, holiday).
Visual idea: A screenshot‑style table with columns “Date,” “Donuts Sold,” “Donuts Left Over,” and “Notes.”

Step 3: Look for Patterns

Now you scan the table and charts to see what tends to repeat.

  • Are Fridays consistently busier than Tuesdays?
  • Do rainy days reduce walk‑in traffic?
  • Do exam weeks on campus increase coffee sales?

This is where your local knowledge matters. You already have a “feel” for busy days — predictive analytics simply helps you confirm and quantify that feeling.

Step 4: Make a Simple Forecast

To forecast, you build a simple rule based on the patterns you observed.

  • Average the last four Fridays to decide how many donuts to prepare this Friday.
  • Use last December’s sales to plan for this December, adjusting if your business has grown.

Analogy: It’s like deciding how early to leave for work based on how long your commute usually takes.

Step 5: Act on the Prediction

Predictions only matter when they change real‑world actions.

  • Prepare fewer items during proven slow hours.
  • Stock extra of the items that usually sell out.
  • Schedule more staff when you expect rush periods.

Step 6: Compare, Learn, and Adjust

After using your prediction for a week or a month, you compare it with what actually happened.

  • Did waste go down?
  • Did you run out of popular items less often?
  • Did sales or profit improve slightly?

You then adjust your rules: maybe the Friday average should ignore one unusual holiday, or maybe you need a different rule for summer vs. winter. This loop — predict, act, review, improve — is the heart of predictive analytics.
